Research

Work in progress:
  • Optimal Tunnelling

    This paper sketches a model that endogenously generates tunnelling as an agent's optimal response to exogenous incentives. An agent who is cognitively constrained by a limited channel of processing capacity may choose to limit her potential of information acquisition by categorically blinding out certain aspects of the world. At the heart of this effect lies an interplay between two systems of attention allocation: a goal-driven/top-down system and a stimulus-driven/bottom-up system. Allowing for a larger impact of the bottom-up component in the attention allocation strategy heightens the agent's capability to react to unusual occurrences in nature but also makes the agent more distractible. I provide a new framework incorporating both systems of attention allocation, which highlights the agent's optimal strategy in reconciling these conflicting forces.

  • Growth Diagnostics: A Foundation

    An influential analytical policy framework used by several policy makers in different countries globally which aims to identify the second-best distortions that constrain the possibilities of achieving socially desirable outcoms is "growth diagnostics", an approach formulated by Hausmann, Rodrik and Velasco (2005). What are the conditions under which growth diagnostics, viewed as an iterative scheme of policy formulation, leads to a socially desirable growth outcome? This paper sets out two such conditions, aligned spillovers and almost surely bounded mistakes, under which such convergence occurs.
